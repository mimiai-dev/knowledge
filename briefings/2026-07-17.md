## Tech

### Moonshot's Kimi K3 becomes the largest open-weight model
**What it is:** Chinese lab Moonshot AI released Kimi K3, a 2.8-trillion-parameter open-weight mixture-of-experts model with a 1-million-token context window, built on two new architecture pieces — Kimi Delta Attention and Attention Residuals — and live now on Kimi.com, Kimi Code, and the Kimi API, with full downloadable weights following on July 27.
**The principle:** K3's mixture-of-experts routing activates only 16 of its 896 experts per token, but sparsity alone isn't new. Moonshot paired it with a redesigned attention mechanism and residual connections that together claim roughly 2.5× better scaling efficiency than its predecessor — meaning each added unit of compute buys more capability than before, not just a bigger parameter count.
**Why it matters:** An open-weight model Moonshot claims is competitive with Anthropic's Fable 5 on some benchmarks narrows the gap between closed frontier labs and freely downloadable models, arriving just ahead of Shanghai's World Artificial Intelligence Conference.
Source: [Kimi (Moonshot AI)](https://www.kimi.com/blog/kimi-k3)

### GitHub Projects gets boolean AND/OR advanced search
**What it is:** GitHub moved the filter bar in Projects views out of preview into general availability, letting teams build views using logical AND and OR across fields instead of the flat, all-must-match filter list Projects shipped with before.
**The principle:** A flat filter list can only narrow a view by stacking conditions that must all hold at once. Boolean grouping lets a query express "match any of these, or match all of those" — a genuinely different query shape, not just a faster way to build the filters the old bar already supported.
**Why it matters:** Large engineering orgs triage thousands of issues through Projects; a saved view can now encode real triage logic — "urgent and unassigned, or blocking a release" — instead of approximating it with several separate views.
Source: [GitHub Changelog](https://github.blog/changelog/2026-07-16-advanced-search-for-projects-is-generally-available/)

### GitHub expands secret scanning to two more services
**What it is:** GitHub's secret scanning added detection for new secret types from partners APIclub and Resend, and started blocking secrets for VolcEngine — ByteDance's cloud platform — from being pushed by default, the latest in a steady stream of push-protection partner additions.
**The principle:** Push protection isn't just text pattern-matching for things that look like keys. Each partner supplies the exact token format its platform issues, so GitHub can match with near-zero false positives and then call the partner's own revocation API to confirm a leaked credential is real before it ever blocks a push, rather than relying on generic heuristics.
**Why it matters:** Every partner added closes a specific blind spot — a credential for that one service that would previously slip through a scan unnoticed until it was already exploited.
Source: [GitHub Changelog](https://github.blog/changelog/2026-07-15-improvements-to-secret-scanning-and-public-monitoring/)

## Science

### Frog protein neutralizes a red tide neurotoxin in mice
**What it is:** UCSF researchers led by Daniel Minor showed that saxiphilin, a protein naturally made by bullfrogs and other frogs, can prevent and even reverse lethal saxitoxin poisoning in mice — saxitoxin being a potent neurotoxin from red-tide algal blooms that currently has no approved antidote and was once stockpiled as a chemical weapon.
**The principle:** Saxitoxin kills by jamming the voltage-gated sodium channels nerve and muscle cells use to fire. Saxiphilin doesn't unblock those channels — it intercepts the toxin in the bloodstream first, binding it tightly enough that it never reaches a channel at all, acting as a molecular sponge rather than a repair mechanism.
**Why it matters:** Saxitoxin accumulates in shellfish during red-tide blooms and has zero approved antidote; a sponge protein that still works after exposure, not just before it, is the first plausible starting point for one.
Source: [UCSF](https://www.ucsf.edu/news/2026/07/432196/ribbit-do-frogs-have-first-antidote-deadly-red-tide-toxin)

### Scientists build a yeast cell with no introns at all
**What it is:** Researchers built a synthetic strain of brewer's yeast with every one of its roughly 300 spliceosomal introns deleted, then showed the cell still grows and divides without any of the core spliceosome components — five small nuclear RNAs and several proteins — that every other known eukaryotic cell needs to survive.
**The principle:** The spliceosome is treated as a defining, essential feature of eukaryotic life because genes are interrupted by introns that must be cut out before their message can be read. Delete every intron in the genome and the job the spliceosome exists to do disappears with it — at which point the machinery itself turns out to be dispensable, not fundamental to survival on its own.
**Why it matters:** It resolves a long-open question about why the spliceosome is universally essential, and hands biologists a clean, intron-free genome as a minimal baseline for studying how introns shaped the evolution of complex genomes.
Source: [Cell](https://www.cell.com/cell/abstract/S0092-8674(26)00624-0)

### A cancer vaccine platform that arms 'cold' tumors
**What it is:** Researchers engineered a lipid nanoparticle mRNA cancer vaccine that co-delivers tumor-antigen mRNA together with two STING immune-pathway agonists — c-di-AMP and manganese ions — packed into the same particle, rather than delivering the antigen alone.
**The principle:** mRNA cancer vaccines often fail against "cold" tumors because presenting an antigen isn't enough — dendritic cells also need a danger signal before they'll mount a T-cell response. Packing two STING agonists into the same particle as the antigen guarantees every cell that takes up the vaccine gets the immune-activating signal and the target at once, instead of hoping antigen and adjuvant separately reach the same cell by chance.
**Why it matters:** Codelivery produced stronger, more durable tumor-specific T-cell responses than antigen-only delivery, offering a more general route to convert immunologically "cold" tumors into ones the immune system can actually attack.
Source: [PNAS](https://www.pnas.org/doi/10.1073/pnas.2525718123)

## World

### Iran's retaliation strikes spread to five Gulf states
**What it is:** As the US intensified its strikes on Iran for a sixth straight night, Iran launched missiles and drones at Bahrain, Kuwait, Jordan, Oman, and Qatar, damaging a power and desalination plant in Kuwait; Jordan said it shot down three incoming missiles with no casualties reported.
**The principle:** The US and Iran are the direct combatants, but neighboring Gulf states hosting no strikes of their own became targets anyway. Once a combatant starts hitting a neighbor's territory and infrastructure rather than only its direct adversary, a bilateral conflict mechanically becomes a regional one — every neighbor now has a reason to intervene, arm up, or seek cover, regardless of its own role in the original dispute.
**Why it matters:** Gulf states depend heavily on desalination for fresh water, so a strike on that plant marks an escalation from military to civilian-infrastructure targeting, raising the humanitarian and diplomatic stakes far beyond the original US-Iran confrontation.

### US convenes 60 nations on 'left-wing' political violence
**What it is:** Secretary of State Marco Rubio convened leaders and officials from more than 60 countries in Washington to coordinate a response to what the Trump administration's 2026 counterterrorism strategy names as a primary threat: violent left-wing extremism, alongside Islamist and narco-terrorism.
**The principle:** A counterterrorism strategy is not a neutral tally of violent incidents — it's a ranked list of what a government has chosen to prioritize. Naming left-wing extremism a primary threat category while leaving right-wing violence out of that top tier makes the strategy itself a political statement about which violence earns a global summit and new visa tools, independent of what the underlying incident data shows.
**Why it matters:** The classification carries real teeth — the State Department has already designated several European antifa-linked groups as foreign terrorist organizations and gained authority to restrict visas — while critics note the focus excludes a category of violence that has historically been more frequent in the US.

### Hong Kong arrests booksellers for the third time in months
**What it is:** Hong Kong police arrested five people linked to two independent bookstores this week for allegedly selling books with "seditious intention" — the third round of bookstore arrests in four months — while a top security official publicly warned booksellers to vet their own stock.
**The principle:** Sedition law has traditionally required proving intent to incite unrest through some direct act. Charging booksellers over books simply present in inventory shifts the point of legal risk from what a person does to what a person merely stocks or sells, making the seller strictly responsible for policing every title on the shelf — collapsing the line between distributing and authoring.
**Why it matters:** Independent bookstores had functioned as one of the last channels for material — like a biography of jailed publisher Jimmy Lai — that state-aligned retailers wouldn't carry; repeated raids under the national security law push that channel toward self-censorship or closure without any single title ever being formally banned.
