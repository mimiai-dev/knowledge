## Edge Tech

### CodeQL adds static detection for AI prompt injection
**What it is:** GitHub's CodeQL 2.26.0 ships a new JavaScript/TypeScript query that flags untrusted data flowing into an LLM's system prompt, with sinks covering OpenAI, Anthropic, and Google GenAI SDK calls, alongside Kotlin 2.4.0 support and accuracy fixes across several languages.

**The principle:** CodeQL finds bugs by tracing dataflow from untrusted sources to sensitive sinks without safe handling in between — the exact technique that catches SQL and command injection. This release just adds a new sink category: a prompt-construction call is now treated the same way a raw SQL query has always been treated.

**Why it matters:** Prompt injection has mostly been caught by manual review or after the fact; folding it into standard static analysis means it gets checked automatically on every commit, the same way a SQL injection bug would be.

Source: https://github.blog/changelog/2026-07-10-codeql-2-26-0-adds-kotlin-2-4-0-support-and-ai-prompt-injection-detection/

### OpenAI retracts its own SWE-Bench Pro endorsement
**What it is:** OpenAI audited SWE-Bench Pro — a coding benchmark it previously urged the field to adopt — using independent engineers who scored each task blind to the submitted solution, and found roughly 30% of tasks broken: bad ground truth, contradictory instructions, or overly strict tests. It is now retracting its recommendation.

**The principle:** A benchmark used to rank models attracts exactly the pressure that erodes it — contamination, sloppy ground truth, tasks nobody re-checks once they're widely cited. An eval only stays trustworthy if someone keeps auditing its ground truth independently of the scores it produces, and that auditing has to happen on a schedule, not just once at launch.

**Why it matters:** A leading lab publicly walking back its own benchmark advice is unusually self-critical, and the blind, human-graded audit method it used is a reusable template for checking whether any widely cited eval still means what it claims to measure.

Source: https://openai.com/index/separating-signal-from-noise-coding-evaluations/

### Meta opens its first paid developer model API
**What it is:** Meta released Muse Spark 1.1, a reasoning model built for coding and agentic tool use, and for the first time opened one of its in-house models to outside developers through a metered Meta Model API compatible with both the OpenAI and Anthropic request formats.

**The principle:** Meta's AI distribution until now has been almost entirely open-weights (Llama): download the model, run it yourself, pay nothing per token. A priced, hosted API for a model you cannot download marks a shift from giving away weights to selling inference directly, the business model the closed labs have run on all along.

**Why it matters:** Speaking the same request format as OpenAI and Anthropic means switching to Muse Spark is a base-URL change, not a rewrite — Meta is competing on switching cost as much as on model quality.

Source: https://ai.meta.com/blog/introducing-muse-spark-meta-model-api/

## Science

### Lab ring mimics a black hole's energy-stealing spin
**What it is:** Physicists built a ring network of resonators modulated in both space and time so it behaves as if spinning at effective faster-than-light angular speed, and in this "Floquet" regime observed rotational super-radiance — waves coming out carrying more energy than they went in with.

**The principle:** In 1971 Zel'dovich predicted a wave scattering off a fast enough spinning absorber can leave amplified, draining rotational energy from the object it bounced off — the same mechanism, classically, that lets the Penrose process pull energy out of a spinning black hole's ergosphere. Modulating a medium in time can substitute for physically spinning it fast enough to test that prediction, which was never mechanically achievable before.

**Why it matters:** A black-hole energy-extraction mechanism that could only ever be theorized about astrophysically is now something a tabletop optics experiment can measure directly.

Source: https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-026-10725-y

### Chip steers light beams in 74 femtoseconds
**What it is:** Caltech engineers built a silicon metasurface chip where one pulse of light redirects a second beam by up to 13 degrees in 74 femtoseconds — roughly a thousand times faster than prior beam-steering technology, and about how long light takes to cross a human hair.

**The principle:** Conventional beam steering is limited by how fast a mirror can physically move or a material's refractive index can be electronically retuned. This chip instead uses the optical Kerr effect in a resonant nanostructure, so the steering speed is set only by how short the pump pulse is, not by any mechanical or thermal response time.

**Why it matters:** Steering at femtosecond speed opens a path to LiDAR and free-space optical links that can track targets or switch channels far faster than any mechanical or slower electro-optic system allows.

Source: https://www.nature.com/articles/s41565-026-02199-w

### CAR-T cells target both a brain tumor and its shield
**What it is:** Researchers found GPNMB, a protein present on both glioblastoma tumor cells and the tumor-associated macrophages that protect them, and engineered CAR-T cells to attack it — producing durable tumor control in patient-derived and mouse glioma models.

**The principle:** Brain tumors resist immunotherapy partly because they recruit their own immune cells to actively suppress attack, shielding the tumor underneath. Targeting a marker shared by the tumor and its protective cells lets one therapy dismantle the shield and the target in the same strike, instead of attacking a tumor the shield is still covering.

**Why it matters:** Many immunotherapy failures trace to a hostile microenvironment rather than the therapy failing to recognize the tumor itself, so a shared target like this could generalize to other solid tumors that hide behind the same kind of defense.

Source: https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-026-10641-1

## World

### DOJ subpoenas NYT reporters over Air Force One story
**What it is:** Four New York Times reporters were subpoenaed by a US Attorney recently nominated as national intelligence director, after the paper reported the Secret Service had urged Trump to avoid flying on the Qatari-donated Air Force One at the NATO summit over security concerns; some subpoenas were delivered to reporters' homes.

**The principle:** A subpoena compelling a journalist to testify about sourcing works as pressure whether or not it ever leads to a charge — the demand for compliance is the mechanism, not a conviction — because a reporter who must either reveal a source or risk contempt has already lost the protection anonymity was supposed to provide.

**Why it matters:** This follows a pattern of US government pressure on other outlets this year, and tests whether the source-protection norms that have shielded journalists for decades still hold when the story embarrasses the administration.

### Israel's Gaza footprint grows nine months into ceasefire
**What it is:** Nine months after the October ceasefire, Israeli forces have steadily expanded control beyond the agreed "Yellow Line," with NPR estimating close to 70% of Gaza under Israeli control and Gaza's Government Media Office citing 80%; an Al Jazeera investigation counted 40 Israeli military outposts inside the strip, eight built since the truce began.

**The principle:** A ceasefire line only holds as well as the cost of crossing it. Without an enforcement mechanism beyond the agreement's own text, incremental territorial expansion can keep outpacing the diplomatic timeline while the occupying force still formally denies the truce has broken.

**Why it matters:** The pace of on-the-ground expansion suggests the ceasefire's promised second phase — withdrawal, disarmament, new governance — has stalled, and the facts being established now will shape whatever follows regardless of the original agreement's terms.

### UN finds genocide in Sudan as a second city is besieged
**What it is:** A new UN investigation concluded that the Rapid Support Forces' mass killings, gang rapes, and deliberate starvation in el-Fasher amount to genocide. Attention has since shifted to el-Obeid, where roughly half a million people are under RSF siege and intensifying drone attack, and on July 11 the EU called for the RSF to be designated a terrorist organization.

**The principle:** A siege applies the same coercive logic as a direct assault, just slower — cutting off food, water, and movement compels surrender or death without storming the city. That is why international law treats deliberate starvation of a besieged population as a war crime in its own right, not a side effect of the surrounding conflict.

**Why it matters:** El-Obeid sits on the corridor between RSF-held Darfur and army-held central Sudan, so its fall could tip a three-year civil war decisively, while the genocide finding raises pressure for an accountability mechanism that has so far been absent.
