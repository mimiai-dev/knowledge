# Daily Knowledge Briefing — 2026-07-16

## Edge Tech

### Thinking Machines releases Inkling, a giant open-weight model
**What it is:** Mira Murati's Thinking Machines Lab released Inkling, its first in-house model: a mixture-of-experts transformer with 975 billion total parameters but only 41 billion active per token, trained on 45 trillion tokens of text, images, audio, and video, with weights open for anyone to download and fine-tune.

**The principle:** A dense model of Inkling's stated size would need to load and compute through nearly a trillion parameters for every token. Mixture-of-experts routes each token to only a small subset of specialized sub-networks, so the model behaves like a much larger system while only paying the compute cost of a much smaller one — that's the trick that lets a 975B-parameter model run at roughly 41B-parameter cost.

**Why it matters:** Open weights mean any company can download, inspect, and fine-tune Inkling directly, rather than renting access to a closed API — intensifying the split between labs racing on frontier closed models and those competing to give enterprises full control of the weights.

Source: https://thinkingmachines.ai/news/introducing-inkling/

### GitHub closes a loophole where 'plan mode' could edit files
**What it is:** GitHub hardened Copilot CLI so that its read-only "plan mode" now hard-blocks any workspace-mutating tool call at the runtime layer, closing a gap where the underlying model could still trigger a file edit mid-planning session despite the mode's label.

**The principle:** Labeling a mode "plan-only" is a promise about what the model is instructed to do, not a guarantee about what it can do. The fix moves the boundary from the model's instructions into the runtime itself, so an edit attempt is refused mechanically, whether or not the model "meant" to make it.

**Why it matters:** As coding agents run with less supervision, this is a small but concrete example of a general safety principle: don't rely on a model to police its own permissions when the enforcement can happen a layer below it instead.

Source: https://github.com/github/copilot-cli/releases/tag/v1.0.71-2

### OpenAI ships a keyboard with a physical reasoning-effort dial
**What it is:** OpenAI's first hardware product, the $230 Codex Micro keyboard built with Work Louder, gives ChatGPT's Codex coding agents a physical control surface: light-up "Agent Keys" showing live status per running agent, a joystick for common workflows, and a dial that sets how much reasoning effort — time and compute — an agent spends on a task.

**The principle:** Modern reasoning models already let a caller trade latency for accuracy by allocating more "thinking" tokens at inference time — normally a hidden parameter in an API call. Turning that trade-off into a twistable physical dial doesn't change the underlying mechanism, it just makes an invisible software knob something a user can feel and set by hand.

**Why it matters:** It's a small but telling sign that "how much should the model think" is becoming a routine, user-facing control across agentic products, not just a backend setting developers tune once.

Source: https://openai.com/supply/co-lab/work-louder/

## Science

### Swallowable robots compute with hydrogels, not chips
**What it is:** Researchers built capsule-sized medical robots for the gastrointestinal tract that sense, decide, and act using mechanical metamaterial memory and hydrogel logic gates — with no batteries, motors, or electronic chips — and validated them in ex vivo and in vivo large-animal trials.

**The principle:** A conventional sensor-response system needs a chip to read a sensor, decide, and command an actuator. Here the "decision" is made by the physical structure itself — a metamaterial that mechanically remembers a state and a hydrogel that performs logic through its own swelling and chemistry — so sensing, computation, and actuation are the same physical event, not three components wired together.

**Why it matters:** Removing electronics from an ingestible device sidesteps battery safety, MRI incompatibility, and the biofouling that plagues implanted electronics — a path toward diagnostic and therapeutic robots simple enough to pass through the body safely.

Source: https://www.pnas.org/doi/10.1073/pnas.2605060123

### Old internet cables become a planet-sized earthquake sensor
**What it is:** Scientists are repurposing deep-sea fiber-optic cables — built for data traffic, not seismology — alongside drilling ships and seafloor sensors to study the ocean crust, a region so unexplored that less than 0.001% of the sea floor has ever been visually surveyed.

**The principle:** A fiber-optic cable already carries pulses of laser light for communication; any physical disturbance to the cable — like the tiny strain from a passing seismic wave — measurably distorts how that light scatters as it travels. Reading that distortion turns thousands of kilometers of cable already lying on the seafloor into one continuous seismic sensor, at zero marginal hardware cost, without ever tapping a byte of the data running through it.

**Why it matters:** The seafloor generates the earthquakes and tsunamis that matter most for coastal warning systems, and this converts already-buried infrastructure into monitoring coverage that would otherwise cost billions of dollars in dedicated ocean sensors.

Source: https://www.nature.com/articles/d41586-026-01988-6

### Computers that compute by letting noise do the work
**What it is:** Quanta Magazine profiles thermodynamic computing, an emerging hardware approach that builds physical devices tuned so close to the thermal energy scale that random thermal fluctuations — the noise every classical chip is engineered to suppress — become the mechanism that drives the computation itself.

**The principle:** A conventional chip spends huge amounts of energy holding transistors in a definite 0-or-1 state against thermal noise trying to flip them. Thermodynamic computing instead lets a system relax naturally toward whichever state minimizes its energy — the same physics by which a protein folds into its lowest-energy shape — so the "answer" to certain optimization problems emerges from letting the system settle, rather than from forcing it through each step.

**Why it matters:** If it scales, it inverts computing's biggest cost driver: instead of paying energy to fight noise, these machines would pay almost nothing and run near room temperature, offering a third paradigm alongside classical and quantum computing for a specific class of hard optimization problems.

Source: https://www.quantamagazine.org/thermodynamic-computers-go-with-the-energy-flow-20260715/

## World

### Zelenskyy fires his most popular minister, sparks protests
**What it is:** President Zelenskyy dismissed Defense Minister Mykhailo Fedorov — the 35-year-old architect of Ukraine's drone program — as part of a cabinet reshuffle that also installed Serhii Koretskyi as prime minister; hundreds protested in Kyiv, Lviv, Odesa, and Dnipro, and the air force's deputy commander resigned over the decision.

**The principle:** Fedorov's removal followed reported friction with Commander-in-Chief Oleksandr Syrskyi over conscription and force-modernization reforms. When a wartime government fires the official credited with a battlefield advantage rather than the one he was clashing with, the move reveals whose institutional position was actually stronger — not whose reforms were working.

**Why it matters:** The reshuffle lands in the middle of an active war effort, and the public backlash — including a resignation inside the military itself — signals real friction over whether Ukraine's modernization drive survives the change.

### Lebanon and Israel agree on the shape of a withdrawal
**What it is:** After a sixth round of US-brokered talks in Rome, Lebanon and Israel agreed on the structure and guidelines for a "pilot zone" process — an initial, verifiable Israeli withdrawal from two designated areas of southern Lebanon, to be followed by Lebanese army deployment and, if it holds, the return of displaced residents.

**The principle:** Neither side trusts the other to honor a single, all-at-once withdrawal after a war and an unresolved disarmament question over Hezbollah. Splitting the exit into small, verifiable pilot zones turns an all-or-nothing trust problem into a repeatable, checkable one — each zone becomes a test case that either builds enough confidence to expand the process or stalls it before further withdrawals are risked.

**Why it matters:** No timetable or exact boundaries were set, so the agreement is a structure for de-escalation, not de-escalation itself — whether "the coming days" produces an actual withdrawal is the real test of whether the framework holds.

### US disables a tanker as Iran threatens a second strait
**What it is:** US Central Command fired Hellfire missiles to disable an oil tanker bound for Iran's Kharg Island terminal, enforcing a reimposed naval blockade, while Iran's Revolutionary Guard threatened to close the Bab el-Mandeb Strait — a second major oil route, this one leading to the Red Sea — in retaliation.

**The principle:** The Strait of Hormuz and the Bab el-Mandeb are two separate chokepoints serving two different sets of oil exporters. Threatening the second strait converts a bilateral US-Iran shipping dispute into a lever against every country that depends on Red Sea shipping — mainly Saudi Arabia — spreading the cost of the conflict onto parties that were not combatants in it.

**Why it matters:** Analysts warn that a simultaneous disruption of both straits could meaningfully damage global oil supply and shipping, which is why the threat alone — without Iran actually acting on it — is enough to move markets and pressure allies not directly involved in the fight.
