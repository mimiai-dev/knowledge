## Tech

### AI agents still stumble on long, multi-step terminal tasks
**What it is:** A new benchmark, Long-Horizon-Terminal-Bench, tests AI coding agents on 46 extended terminal tasks — reproducing a published experiment, debugging a multi-stage pipeline, running scientific computing workflows — grading not just the final answer but every intermediate step. Across 17 frontier models, the best performer, Grok 4.5, solved only 19.6% of tasks perfectly, despite averaging 9.8 million tokens and 89 minutes of execution per attempt.

**The principle:** Most agent benchmarks score only whether the final answer was right, which reveals nothing about where a failing agent actually went wrong. Decomposing each long task into graded subtasks gives partial credit for correct intermediate progress, turning a single pass/fail bit into a trace of exactly which step in a multi-hour workflow the agent lost the thread.

**Why it matters:** It's evidence that today's frontier models, however fluent on short well-specified tasks, still can't reliably sustain a multi-hour, multi-step plan — the exact capability needed for agents to run real engineering work unsupervised.

Source: [arXiv](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.08964)

### OpenAI defends teen access to ChatGPT with age-prediction safeguards
**What it is:** OpenAI published its reasoning for expanding rather than restricting teen access to ChatGPT, noting nearly 9 in 10 teens who use it weekly do so for learning, research, or skill-building. Its approach leans on an automated age-prediction system, expanded parental controls, and a "Study Mode" that walks students through problems with guiding questions instead of handing over direct answers.

**The principle:** A blanket age gate can only be as strict as its most restrictive user, blocking legitimate learning use just to stop a minority of misuse. An age-prediction model instead estimates a likely age band from usage patterns and applies different default safeguards per band, letting the same product serve a 10-year-old and an adult under different rules without asking either to prove who they are.

**Why it matters:** It's a concrete bet that inferred-age personalization, not hard verification or blanket restriction, is how a general-purpose AI product can keep serving a legitimately large teen learning audience while still tightening safety defaults.

Source: [OpenAI](https://openai.com/index/why-teens-deserve-access-safe-ai/)

### GitHub's vulnerability reports quintupled as AI speeds disclosure
**What it is:** GitHub disclosed that its Advisory Database published 1,560 reviewed advisories in a single month this year — more than five times its typical output — as private vulnerability reports rose from about 550 a week in January to over 3,000 a week by May, and CVE requests climbed almost tenfold year-over-year.

**The principle:** A disclosure pipeline sized for a steady trickle of reports breaks when the trickle becomes a flood, since expert review time doesn't scale down just because report volume scales up. GitHub's fix was to give curators AI-assisted tooling that speeds up an advisory's fact-gathering step — checking affected versions, prior CVEs, exploit details — while keeping the actual accept/reject judgment human, splitting the pipeline into a machine-scaled search phase and a human-scaled decision phase.

**Why it matters:** The surge itself is a signal that AI-assisted vulnerability hunting is now finding real bugs faster than human-only triage can process them, and how that bottleneck gets resolved will shape how fast the open-source ecosystem learns about flaws in the code it depends on.

Source: [GitHub Blog](https://github.blog/security/supply-chain-security/inside-the-advisory-database-and-what-happens-when-vulnerability-volume-breaks-records/)

## Science

### A 2024 fireball meteorite carried brines that could seed life
**What it is:** Scientists analyzed a meteorite that fell as a daytime fireball over the New York City area, finding it contains CM1 clasts — fragments rich in water and sodium — laced with amino acids and other organic-chemistry byproducts, preserved in briny pockets from its parent asteroid.

**The principle:** Building the molecules of life from scratch requires phosphate to stay dissolved long enough to react with organics and minerals, but phosphate normally locks into insoluble rock. A salty brine keeps phosphate in solution instead, and this meteorite is direct physical evidence that exactly this kind of chemistry-sustaining brine formed and persisted inside an asteroid, not just in origin-of-life lab simulations.

**Why it matters:** It's a rare case where a real extraterrestrial sample, rather than a lab reconstruction, backs up a specific proposed pathway from asteroid chemistry to prebiotic molecules — including rare amino acids not typically made by life on Earth.

Source: [Science Advances](https://www.science.org/doi/10.1126/sciadv.aea2105)

### New RNA-imaging method maps gene activity inside intact tissue
**What it is:** Researchers combined an RNA-amplification step with MERFISH, an error-correcting imaging technique, to image roughly 33,000 distinct RNAs — about 23,000 genes and 10,000 of their splice-variant isoforms — directly inside individual cells within intact mouse brain tissue, rather than in cells dissociated from it.

**The principle:** Standard single-cell sequencing has to break tissue apart to read RNA, destroying exactly the spatial arrangement that determines which cells are signaling to which neighbors. Amplifying each RNA molecule in place before imaging it makes even rare transcripts and their specific splice variants bright enough to detect without removing cells from their native tissue context, so location and identity are captured in the same measurement instead of being traded off against each other.

**Why it matters:** Resolving isoforms, not just genes, in intact tissue lets researchers map which specific version of a gene's protein a cell is making based on where it physically sits — a level of detail standard spatial transcriptomics discards.

Source: [Cell](https://www.cell.com/cell/fulltext/S0092-8674(26)00747-6)

### Tau-lowering drug slows Alzheimer's where antibody drugs failed
**What it is:** A phase 2 trial of diranersen, a drug that reduces the brain's own production of tau protein, found that after 18 months patients had between one-third and one-half as much tau in their spinal fluid as when they started, with brain imaging showing fewer of the tangles tau forms inside neurons as Alzheimer's progresses.

**The principle:** Most tau-targeting Alzheimer's drugs so far have been antibodies designed to clear out tau tangles after they've already formed, and nearly all of those have failed in trials. Diranersen instead works upstream, using an antisense oligonucleotide to block the cell's machinery from producing tau messenger RNA in the first place, so there's simply less tau protein made to ever tangle.

**Why it matters:** It's the clearest clinical evidence yet that shutting off tau production, rather than mopping it up afterward, can measurably slow the disease — reviving a drug target that a string of antibody failures had made many researchers give up on.

Source: [Science](https://www.science.org/content/article/alzheimer-s-trial-results-lend-momentum-strategies-targeting-tau)

## World

### Iran war escalates to a nuclear site and Kuwait's water supply
**What it is:** On the eighth consecutive night of US strikes on Iran, American forces hit an under-construction Iranian nuclear power plant, while Iran struck a Kuwaiti power and desalination plant for the second time in two days — a country that draws roughly 90% of its water from desalination. The exchange followed the deaths of two US soldiers in an Iranian strike on a base in Jordan.

**The principle:** Desalination and nuclear-power infrastructure are civilian systems, but they are also singular points of failure — destroy the one plant a country's water or power supply depends on, and there's no fast substitute. Once strikes move from military targets to this kind of infrastructure, damage compounds through an entire population instead of staying contained to combatants, which is why targeting it draws a different order of international alarm than a strike on a base does.

**Why it matters:** A war that began between two countries is now directly endangering the water supply of a third, non-combatant Gulf state, showing how strikes on shared regional infrastructure spread a conflict's costs far beyond the parties actually fighting it.

### Russia fires its largest missile barrage of the war on Kyiv
**What it is:** Russia launched 41 missiles — including 25 ballistic missiles, its largest ballistic-missile count of the war according to Ukraine's foreign minister — plus 125 attack drones at Kyiv overnight, killing one person and wounding at least 16 across six districts. Ukrainian air defense intercepted 108 of the drones and 18 of the missiles, shooting down 17 ballistic missiles specifically.

**The principle:** A ballistic missile flies a fast, largely unpowered arc that's far harder to intercept than a slow-moving drone, so a country's missile-defense success rate is really a measure of how many advanced interceptors it still has in stock. Ukraine intercepting most of Saturday's ballistic missiles suggests its interceptor stockpile has been replenished since the US granted Kyiv a license to manufacture its own Patriot interceptors earlier this month, turning a supply-chain decision into a measurable battlefield outcome weeks later.

**Why it matters:** The record missile count shows Russia still has the capacity to escalate its air war on Kyiv at will, while Ukraine's interception rate is a live test of whether its newly localized interceptor supply can actually keep pace.

### WHO: Congo's Ebola toll may be four times the official count
**What it is:** The World Health Organization said the real scale of the DR Congo Ebola outbreak — officially over 2,000 cases and nearly 800 deaths — is likely two to four times the confirmed tally, with 80% of new cases traced to unknown chains of transmission. The outbreak, caused by the rarer Bundibugyo strain, has no approved vaccine, unlike the more common Zaire strain.

**The principle:** An outbreak's official case count only measures what surveillance actually catches, and once most new infections can't be traced back to a known chain, the gap between confirmed and actual cases compounds rather than closes. WHO's 2-4x multiplier is a modeled correction for that untraceable majority — a sign the outbreak's true spread is running well ahead of the health system's ability to see it, not just contain it.

**Why it matters:** This is already the third-largest Ebola outbreak on record, reaching 2,000 cases in two months versus over ten months for a comparable 2018-2020 outbreak, and it's spreading through a strain the field's only approved vaccine doesn't cover.
